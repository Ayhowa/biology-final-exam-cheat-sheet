12 DNA Technology & Genomics
Core Tools
  • Restriction enzymes – cut DNA at specific sequences; enable "cut & paste"
  • DNA ligase – pastes DNA fragments together
  • Plasmids – bacterial DNA rings used as vectors to carry genes
  • cDNA – made from mRNA via reverse transcriptase; no introns
  • Nucleic acid probes – short, labeled ssDNA that tags specific sequences
PCR – Polymerase Chain Reaction
Denature (heat) → Primers anneal → DNA polymerase copies → Repeat ×30+
  • Amplifies tiny DNA samples exponentially
  • Specific primers target only desired sequence
Cloning a Gene
Cut gene + plasmid → Ligate → recombinant plasmid → Insert into bacterium → Express protein
CRISPR-Cas9 Gene Editing
  • Guide RNA targets specific DNA sequence
  • Cas9 protein cuts both strands
  • Can knock out, repair, or replace genes in living cells
  • Applications: treating DMD, cancer, crop improvement
GMOs & Applications
  • Recombinant bacteria produce insulin, growth hormone, vaccines
  • Transgenic crops – Bt corn (insect resistance), herbicide-tolerant plants
  • Gene therapy – correcting defective genes in somatic cells (still limited success)
DNA Profiling
  • STRs (short tandem repeats) – repeated sequences unique to individuals
  • Gel electrophoresis – separates DNA by size (shorter = travels farther)
  • Uses: forensics, paternity, identifying remains
Genomics & Bioinformatics
  • Genomics – study of whole genomes
  • Human Genome – ~21,000 genes; majority is noncoding DNA
  • Whole-genome shotgun – sequence many short fragments, then assemble
  • Bioinformatics – computational analysis of DNA/protein sequences
  • Comparing genomes reveals evolutionary relationships
13 How Populations Evolve
Key Terms
  • Population – group of same species in same area
  • Gene pool – all alleles in a population
  • Microevolution – change in allele frequencies over generations
  • Hardy-Weinberg – no evolution if: large pop, random mating, no mutation/migration/selection
Causes of Microevolution
  • Natural selection – differential reproductive success based on heritable traits
  • Genetic drift – random changes in allele frequency (strongest in small pops)
  • Bottleneck effect – disaster reduces population → lost genetic diversity
  • Founder effect – small group colonizes new area → limited gene pool
  • Gene flow – migration moves alleles between populations
  • Mutation – ultimate source of new alleles
Types of Natural Selection
TypeEffectExample
DirectionalShifts phenotype toward one extremeDrug resistance in bacteria
StabilizingFavors intermediate phenotypeHuman birth weight
DisruptiveFavors both extremes over middleBeak sizes in birds
Variation & Preservation
  • Sexual reproduction shuffles alleles → diversity
  • Balancing selection – maintains multiple alleles (e.g., heterozygote advantage)
  • Heterozygote advantage – sickle-cell heterozygotes survive malaria better than either homozygote
  • Diploidy – recessive alleles "hidden" in heterozygotes, preserved in gene pool
Sexual Selection
  • Sexual dimorphism – physical differences between sexes
  • Intrasexual – same sex compete (male-male combat)
  • Intersexual – female chooses mate; elaborate traits signal "good genes"
  • E.g., peacock feathers, frog mating calls, elk antlers
Limits of Natural Selection
  • Works only on existing variation
  • Constrained by historical (ancestral) structures
  • Adaptations are compromises; chance also affects outcomes
  • Cannot produce perfect organisms
Drug Resistance (Evolution in Action)
  • Antibiotics kill susceptible bacteria; resistant mutants survive & reproduce
  • Overuse of antibiotics accelerates resistance evolution
  • Resistant strains spread; older drugs become ineffective
19 Vertebrate Diversity & Evolution
Chordate Phylogeny
Lancelets → Tunicates → Vertebrates → Jawed → Tetrapods → Amniotes
  • Vertebrates – distinguished by vertebral column + skull
  • Amniotes – tetrapods with terrestrially-adapted egg (reptiles + mammals)
Jawless Fish
  • Hagfishes – produce massive slime; scavenge; tie themselves in knots
  • Lampreys – parasitic; rasp host's blood; invaded Great Lakes via canals
  • Notochord = main support; vertebrae are simple cartilage prongs
Jawed Vertebrates (Fishes)
GroupKey Feature
Chondrichthyes (sharks, rays)Cartilage skeleton; electrosensors; lateral line
Ray-finned fishesBone; operculum; swim bladder; 27,000+ species
Lobe-finsRod-shaped fin bones; lungs; gave rise to tetrapods
Fish → Tetrapod Transition
Eusthenopteron (fish) → Panderichthys → Tiktaalik (fin+foot) → Acanthostega (fishlike tetrapod) → Ichthyostega (land-capable)
  • Tiktaalik: had neck + sturdy fins; perfectly intermediate form
  • First tetrapods were fishlike, not fish dragging onto land
Amphibians, Reptiles, Birds, Mammals
  • Amphibians – moist skin for gas exchange; eggs need water; tadpole metamorphosis; ~1/3 threatened with extinction
  • Reptiles (amniotes) – amniotic egg; keratin scales; ectothermic; includes lizards, snakes, turtles, crocs, and birds
  • Amniotic egg – amnion (fluid), yolk sac, chorion (O₂), allantois (waste), albumen, shell
  • Birds – evolved from theropod dinosaurs; endothermic; hollow bones; feathers for insulation → flight; Archaeopteryx = oldest bird fossil
  • Mammals – hair + mammary glands; endothermic; 4-chambered heart
Mammal Lineages
GroupReproductionExample

Primate & Human Evolution
  • Primates – grasping hands, forward eyes, flexible joints; arboreal origin
  • Anthropoids – monkeys + apes; opposable thumb
  • Apes – gibbons, orangutans, gorillas, chimpanzees, humans; no tail; larger brain
  • Hominins – human branch; bipedal; ~20 fossil species found
  • Australopiths – bipedal but small brain (~450 cm³); e.g., A. afarensis (Lucy)
  • Homo habilis – first Homo; used stone tools; 510–690 cm³ brain
  • H. erectus – first to leave Africa; 940 cm³; sophisticated tools
  • Neanderthals – large brained; interbred with H. sapiens; extinct 40–28 kya
  • H. sapiens – originated Africa ~160–200 kya; 1,300 cm³ brain; symbolic thought
  • Bipedalism evolved first; enlarged brain came later
24 The Immune System
Two Lines of Defense
INNATEADAPTIVE
SpeedImmediateDays–weeks
SpecificityBroad (general)Highly specific
MemoryNoYes
Found inAll animalsVertebrates only
Innate Immunity
External Barriers
  • Skin, mucous membranes, cilia, acidic secretions
Internal Defenses
  • Phagocytes – engulf & digest pathogens via phagocytosis
  • Neutrophils – most abundant WBC; enter tissues at infection
  • Macrophages – "big eaters"; wander tissues; also present antigens
  • Natural killer cells – destroy cancer & virus-infected cells
  • Interferons – proteins from virus-infected cells; warn neighbors
  • Complement system – ~30 proteins; cascade → lyse invaders
Inflammatory Response
Injury/infection → Mast cells release histamine → Capillaries dilate & become leaky → Neutrophils migrate in → Phagocytosis → healing
  • Signs: redness, heat, swelling, pain
  • Chronic inflammation linked to arthritis, heart disease, cancer
  • Septic shock = system-wide inflammatory response; dangerous
Adaptive Immunity
  • Antigen – any foreign molecule that triggers an immune response
  • Antibody – Y-shaped protein; binds one specific antigen
  • Epitope – specific region on antigen recognized by antibody/receptor
  • B cells → mature in bone marrow → HUMORAL response (antibodies in blood/lymph)
  • T cells → mature in thymus → CELL-MEDIATED response (attack infected cells)
Clonal Selection
Antigen enters body → Binds matching B/T cell → Cell proliferates (clone) → Effector cells (fight now) + Memory cells (protect later)
Primary vs Secondary Response
FeaturePrimarySecondary
Trigger1st exposure2nd+ exposure
SpeedSlow (days)Fast (hours)
Antibody levelLowHigh & prolonged
BasisFew lymphocytesMemory cells
  • Vaccination = inducing primary response safely → memory → rapid secondary if real pathogen appears
Helper T Cells (Central Role)
  • Recognize self-nonself complex on antigen-presenting cells
  • Release signaling molecules that activate both B cells AND cytotoxic T cells
  • Without helper T cells → virtually no immune response
  • HIV destroys helper T cells → both humoral & cell-mediated responses fail → AIDS
Cytotoxic T Cells
  • Recognize infected cells displaying self-nonself complex
  • Release perforin → punches holes in infected cell membrane → cell dies
  • Also target cancer cells infected with viruses (HPV, hepatitis B)
Antibody Function (24.9)
  • Neutralization – blocks viral binding sites; coats bacteria
  • Phagocytosis enhancement – antibody-coated pathogens easier to engulf
  • Complement activation – punctures foreign cell membranes
Immune Disorders
  • Autoimmune – immune system attacks self (lupus, rheumatoid arthritis, MS, type 1 diabetes)
  • Immunodeficiency – immune response absent/defective (SCID = no B or T cells; AIDS = HIV depletes helper T)
  • Allergy – overreaction to harmless antigen (allergen); mast cells release histamine
  • Anaphylactic shock – life-threatening allergy; treated with epinephrine (EpiPen)
  • MHC molecules – "molecular fingerprints"; why organ transplants are rejected
Herd Immunity & HIV
  • Herd immunity – enough vaccinated people protect even the unvaccinated
  • Flu requires ~70% vaccination for herd immunity
  • HIV mutates rapidly (reverse transcriptase lacks proofreading) → drug resistance; no vaccine yet
  • Active immunity = body produces own antibodies | Passive = receive premade antibodies (temporary)
