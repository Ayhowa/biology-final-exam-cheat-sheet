Chapter 12
DNA Technology & Genomics
Gene cloning, editing, GMOs, DNA profiling, genomics
Key Terms
Biotechnology
Manipulation of organisms or their components for useful products
Recombinant DNA
DNA formed by combining pieces from two different organisms
Plasmid
Small circular bacterial DNA used as a cloning vector (gene carrier)
Vector
DNA carrier (e.g. plasmid) used to introduce foreign gene into a cell
Restriction Enzyme
Bacterial enzyme that cuts DNA at specific short sequences (restriction sites)
DNA Ligase
"Pasting" enzyme that joins two DNA fragments with covalent bonds
Sticky Ends
Single-stranded overhangs left after restriction enzyme cuts; allow complementary bonding
cDNA
Complementary DNA made from mRNA using reverse transcriptase; no introns
CRISPR-Cas9
Guide RNA directs Cas9 protein to cut specific DNA sequence for editing
GMO
Organism with genes altered by artificial means; transgenic if foreign gene added
Gene Therapy
Alteration of diseased individual's genes for therapeutic purposes
Genomics
Scientific study of whole genomes and their functions
Gene Cloning in Plasmids — Steps
1
Isolate plasmid from bacterium (vector) + source DNA containing gene of interest
2
Cut both with the same restriction enzyme → creates matching sticky ends
3
Mix fragments; complementary sticky ends base-pair; DNA ligase joins them → recombinant plasmid
4
Introduce recombinant plasmid into bacterium (transformation)
5
Bacterium reproduces → clone of cells, each carrying the target gene
6
Harvest gene copies or the protein product (e.g. insulin, growth hormone)
CRISPR-Cas9 Gene Editing
1
Guide RNA is designed to be complementary to target DNA sequence
2
Guide RNA + Cas9 protein complex enters cell
3
Cas9 binds to target DNA, cuts both strands
4
Cell's repair enzymes re-join cut — can knock out gene or insert normal copy
Key diff from cloning: Gene cloning copies a gene exactly; CRISPR-Cas9 modifies a gene inside a living cell. Cas9 can cut any sequence directed by guide RNA.
DNA Profiling (Forensics)
PCR
Polymerase Chain Reaction — amplifies specific DNA segment billions of times using primers + heat-stable polymerase. 3 steps: Heat → Cool → Extend (repeat)
Gel Electrophoresis
Separates DNA fragments by size. DNA (neg. charge) moves toward positive pole. Shorter = faster
STR Analysis
Short Tandem Repeats — compare # of repeats at ~13 sites. Match probability <1 in 10 billion. Stored in CODIS database
Nucleic Acid Probe
Labeled single-stranded DNA that base-pairs with complementary sequence to locate specific gene
Medical Applications of DNA Technology
Treating disease: Human insulin (1982 — first recombinant drug), growth hormone (HGH), TPA (dissolves blood clots)
Diagnosing disease: DNA analysis identifies sickle-cell, hemophilia, cystic fibrosis, Huntington's — even before symptoms
Vaccines: Yeast engineered to produce hepatitis B surface protein; harmless viral mutants used as live vaccines
Gene therapy: Normal allele inserted via retrovirus into patient's cells. Success in SCID (bubble boy disease). Risk: viral DNA may cause cancer
Genomics & The Human Genome
HGP (Human Genome Project): Completed 2003. ~3 billion bp; ~21,000 genes; only ~1.5% codes for protein
Composition: Introns 19%, Repetitive DNA 44+15%, Regulatory sequences 5%, Unique noncoding DNA 15%
Whole-genome shotgun method: Chop genome, sequence all fragments, use software to reassemble overlapping sequences
Bioinformatics: Computational analysis of biological data; NCBI/GenBank; BLAST tool compares sequences across species
Humans vs. chimps: Genomes differ by only ~1.2% in single-base substitutions + 2.7% insertions/deletions

Chapter 13
How Populations Evolve
Darwin, evidence for evolution, population genetics, microevolution
Darwin's Theory
HMS Beagle voyage (1831–1836): Darwin observed geographic distribution — South American species resembled each other, not European ones. Galápagos finches & tortoises varied island to island
Two main ideas: (1) Species change over time — descent with modification; (2) Mechanism = natural selection
Natural selection logic: Overproduction → Variation → Heredity → Differential survival = individuals with adaptive traits survive and reproduce more
3 Key points: Populations evolve (not individuals); only heritable traits selected; evolution is not goal-directed
Evidence for Evolution
Fossil record: Chronological record in rock strata. Shows transitional forms (e.g., whale ancestors: Pakicetus → Ambulocetus → Rodhocetus → Dorudon → modern whale)
Homologous structures: Same skeletal elements (humerus, radius, ulna) in human, cat, whale, bat forelegs — different functions, same common ancestry
Vestigial structures: Remnants of ancestral features (pelvic bones in whales, appendix)
Molecular homology: All life uses same genetic code. Related species have more similar DNA. Universal genetic code = strong evidence of common ancestor
Direct observation: Pesticide resistance in insects; finch beak size changes (Grants, Galápagos); antibiotic resistance in bacteria
Evolutionary tree (13.5): Branch points = common ancestors. Hatch marks show shared derived characters. Tetrapods share limbs; amniotes share amnion; birds share feathers
Population Genetics
Population
Group of same species in same area that can interbreed
Gene Pool
All allele copies at every locus in all members of a population
Microevolution
Change in allele frequencies in a gene pool over generations
Mutation
Ultimate source of new alleles; random; usually harmful; only heritable if in gametes
Hardy-Weinberg Equilibrium
H-W equation: p² + 2pq + q² = 1 (genotype frequencies) and p + q = 1 (allele frequencies). A population NOT evolving stays in H-W equilibrium.
Hardy-Weinberg Equation
p² + 2pq + q² = 1
5 conditions for equilibrium (must ALL be met): Very large population · No gene flow · No mutations · Random mating · No natural selection
Real populations rarely meet all 5 → microevolution occurs. H-W used to detect evolution and calculate carrier frequencies (e.g., PKU: q² = 1/10,000 → q = 0.01 → 2pq ≈ 2% carriers)
Mechanisms of Microevolution
Natural Selection
Only mechanism that consistently leads to adaptive evolution. Acts on phenotype; heritable traits change in frequency
Genetic Drift
Random changes in allele frequency; more impact in small populations. Types: bottleneck effect, founder effect
Gene Flow
Movement of alleles between populations when individuals migrate; reduces differences between populations
Bottleneck Effect
Disaster drastically reduces population → survivors have reduced genetic variation (e.g., cheetahs, prairie chickens)
Founder Effect
Small group colonizes new area → limited gene pool. Explains high frequency of certain disorders in isolated populations
Relative Fitness
Contribution an individual makes to the gene pool relative to others = number of fertile offspring
Types of Natural Selection
TypeEffect on PopulationExample
DirectionalShifts phenotype distribution toward one extremeCliff swallows — larger bodies survived cold
StabilizingFavors intermediate phenotypes; removes extremesHuman birth weight (6–8 lbs optimal)
DisruptiveFavors both extremes over intermediateAfrican finches with small or large beaks
SexualTraits that improve mating success (sexual dimorphism)Peacock tail; male elk antlers
Heterozygote advantage (balancing selection): Sickle-cell heterozygotes are protected from malaria AND don't suffer severe sickle-cell disease → both alleles maintained in population in malaria regions
Natural selection cannot fashion perfect organisms because: acts only on existing variation; limited by historical constraints; adaptations are compromises; chance/environment interact

Evolution of Vertebrate Diversity
Vertebrate clades, fish → tetrapods, mammals, primates, human evolution
Vertebrate Phylogeny — Key Transitions
1
Vertebral column → distinguishes vertebrates from other chordates
2
Jaws → evolved from gill-support cartilage; opened new feeding niches
3
Lungs/lung derivatives → shared derived character of ray-fins + lobe-fins
4
Lobed fins (rod-shaped bones) → precursor to tetrapod limbs
5
Tetrapod limbs (legs) → transition to land; amphibians first
6
Amniotic egg → watertight; allowed complete life cycle on land; reptiles + mammals
7
Milk (mammary glands + hair) → distinguishes mammals
Major Vertebrate Groups
GroupKey Features
Hagfishes & LampreysJawless; notochord main support in adult; lampreys are parasites; hagfish produce slime
Chondrichthyans (sharks/rays)Cartilage skeleton; lateral line system; electrosensors; must swim to breathe
Ray-finned fishesBony skeleton; operculum; swim bladder (buoyancy); >27,000 species
Lobe-finned fishesRod-shaped bones in muscular fins; coelacanth, lungfish, tetrapods
AmphibiansTetrapods; moist skin for gas exchange; eggs in water; tadpole stage; first land vertebrates
Reptiles (incl. birds)Amniotic egg; dry scaly skin; ectothermic (except birds); dinosaur descendants survive as birds
BirdsFeathered reptiles; endothermic; 4-chambered heart; hollow bones; evolved from theropod dinosaurs
MammalsHair; mammary glands; endothermic; monotremes lay eggs; marsupials have pouch; eutherians = placental
Tetrapod Evolution — Key Fossils
Eusthenopteron (385 mya): Fish with rod-shaped fin bones; lung-like structures
Panderichthys (385 mya): Fish-like but crocodile-shaped head; fins with fish-like rays; had lungs + gills
Tiktaalik (375 mya): "Large freshwater fish" — part fin/part foot; tetrapod-like neck; could prop itself on bottom; perfectly intermediate
Acanthostega (365 mya): Fishlike tetrapod; neck + 4 limbs with toes but couldn't support weight on land; showed first tetrapods were aquatic
Ichthyostega (365 mya): Advanced tetrapod features; flipper-like hind limbs; powerful shoulders
Primate Diversity
Primate Features
Short snout; forward-facing eyes; limber joints; five mobile digits; flexible thumb — adaptations for tree life
Anthropoids
Monkeys + apes; fully opposable thumb; larger brain. Old World monkeys (Africa/Asia) vs. New World (Americas)
Apes
No tail; long arms; large brains. Gibbons, orangutans, gorillas, chimpanzees, humans
Hominins
Species more closely related to humans than chimps; ~20 known extinct species. Human + chimp lineages diverged ~6–7 mya
Human Evolution — Hominin Timeline
Sahelanthropus tchadensis (~6.5 mya): Oldest known hominin; may have walked upright
Ardipithecus ramidus (~4.4 mya): Woodland creature; walked upright AND in trees; small brain
Australopithecus afarensis (~3.2 mya): "Lucy" — bipedal; small brain (~same as chimp); long curved fingers; walked for ≥1 million years. Laetoli footprints confirm bipedalism
Homo habilis ("handy man", ~2.4–1.6 mya): First Homo; brain 510–690 cm³; crude stone tools
H. ergaster (~1.9–1.0 mya): Brain 750–850 cm³; modern proportions; sophisticated tools; first to leave Africa
H. erectus (~1.8 mya–200,000 ya): Brain ~940 cm³; spread to Asia; found in Georgia, China, Indonesia
H. neanderthalensis (~350,000–28,000 ya): Brain larger than ours; hunted big game; tools; Europeans/Asians carry ~2% Neanderthal DNA
H. sapiens (~200,000 ya–present): Brain ~1,300 cm³; originated in Africa; evidence = fossil + mitochondrial DNA + Y chromosome analysis
Key insight: Bipedalism came FIRST (>4 mya), brain enlargement came MUCH LATER (~2 mya). Bipedalism is the initial key derived character of hominins.

The Immune System
Innate immunity, adaptive immunity, lymphocytes, antibodies, disorders
Two Branches of Immunity
FeatureInnate ImmunityAdaptive Immunity
SpeedImmediate (always ready)Slow (days to activate)
SpecificityBroad (general pathogens)Highly specific (one antigen)
MemoryNoneYes — decades-long
Found inAll animalsVertebrates only
Key cellsPhagocytes, NK cellsB cells, T cells (lymphocytes)
Innate Immunity
External Barriers

Skin, acidic secretions, mucous membranes, cilia (sweep pathogens out), microbiota balance

Internal Defenses

Phagocytes (neutrophils + macrophages), natural killer cells, interferons, complement system, inflammatory response

Phagocytosis: Pathogen engulfed → vacuole forms → lysosome fuses → enzymes destroy pathogen → debris released by exocytosis
Natural Killer Cells: Recognize & destroy cancer cells and virus-infected cells; release chemicals causing cell death
Interferons: Proteins released by virus-infected cells; trigger nearby cells to produce substances that inhibit viral replication
Complement System: ~30 proteins; activated by pathogen surfaces; causes cell lysis, enhances phagocytosis, recruits more immune cells
Inflammatory Response (24.2)
1
Tissue injury → bacteria activate macrophages → release signaling molecules → mast cells release histamine
2
Histamine causes blood capillaries to dilate and become leaky → fluid + proteins move into tissue → redness, heat, swelling
3
Neutrophils squeeze out of leaky capillaries, enter tissue, engulf bacteria and cell debris
4
Clotting proteins + platelets form clot to seal infection; neutrophils die → pus; tissue heals
Chronic inflammation associated with arthritis, heart disease, Alzheimer's, some cancers. Septic shock = overwhelming systemic inflammation → fluid accumulation, low blood pressure, organ failure
Adaptive Immunity — Key Terms
Antigen
Foreign molecule that triggers adaptive response. Antigens have multiple epitopes (binding sites)
Antibody
Immune protein (Y-shaped, 4 polypeptide chains) that binds specific antigen. Made by B cells. Marks invaders for destruction
B Cells
Mature in bone marrow; humoral response; secrete antibodies into blood/lymph against free-floating antigens
T Cells
Mature in thymus; cell-mediated response (helper T) or cytotoxic T cells that destroy infected cells
Helper T Cell
Triggers both humoral AND cell-mediated responses. Recognizes self-nonself complex on antigen-presenting cell (macrophage)
Cytotoxic T Cell
Destroys infected body cells. Releases perforin → holes in membrane → enzymes kill cell. Also fights some cancers
MHC Molecules
Major histocompatibility complex — self proteins on cell surfaces; molecular "fingerprints"; >100 alleles → unique to each person (except identical twins)
Antigen-Presenting Cell
Macrophage or B cell displays self-nonself complex to helper T cell to activate immune response
Clonal Selection (24.7)
1
Antigen enters lymph node; binds only to B/T cells with matching antigen receptors
2
Selected cell is activated → proliferates → forms a clone of thousands of identical cells
3
Clone differentiates into effector cells (fight current infection; live 4–5 days) AND memory cells (last decades)
4
Effector B cells → secrete 2,000 antibodies/second. Memory cells wait for next exposure
Primary vs. Secondary Immune Response
FeaturePrimary ResponseSecondary Response
WhenFirst exposure to antigenRe-exposure to same antigen
SpeedSlow (days to weeks)Fast (starts within days)
Antibody levelLower peakMuch higher peak
DurationShorterLonger/more prolonged
Why fasterFew lymphocytes with right receptorLarge pool of memory cells already present
Vaccination: Vaccine (harmless antigen) induces primary response + memory cells. Real pathogen → rapid secondary response before illness develops. This is active immunity. Passive immunity = premade antibodies injected (antivenom); temporary, no memory cells
Antibody Function (24.9)
Neutralization: Antibodies block viral binding sites or coat bacteria → prevent infection + enhance phagocytosis
Complement activation: Antibody-bound antigens activate complement proteins → membrane attack complexes → cell lysis (rupture)
Structure: Y-shaped; 4 polypeptide chains; 2 antigen-binding sites at tips; stem helps dispose of bound antigen
Disorders of the Immune System
HIV/AIDS
HIV destroys helper T cells → both humoral + cell-mediated responses fail → opportunistic infections (e.g., P. carinii pneumonia, Kaposi's sarcoma). Transmitted via body fluids
Autoimmune Diseases
Immune system attacks body's own molecules. Examples: lupus (antibodies vs. DNA/histones), rheumatoid arthritis (synovium), MS (myelin sheath), type 1 diabetes (pancreatic cells)
Allergies
Overreaction to harmless allergens (pollen, pet proteins). Stage 1: sensitization — B cells make antibodies that attach to mast cells. Stage 2: allergen binds mast cell → histamine release → symptoms
Anaphylactic Shock
Severe allergic reaction; massive histamine → airways constrict, blood vessels dilate → dangerous drop in blood pressure. Treated with epinephrine (EpiPen)
Immunodeficiency
Underreaction; immune response defective or absent. SCID = no functional B or T cells. Acquired immunodeficiency: AIDS, Hodgkin's, chemotherapy
Antihistamines
Drugs that block histamine action; give temporary relief from allergy symptoms
HIV rapid evolution: Reverse transcriptase lacks proofreading → high mutation rate → drug-resistant strains evolve rapidly. Patients given multidrug regimens to combat this. Drug-resistant strains now transmitted to newly infected patients
